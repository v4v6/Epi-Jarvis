# Epi-Jarvis-Cal — Project brief

## Concept
A California-first epidemiological intelligence dashboard inspired by real-time geopolitical terminal design, but optimized for public health surveillance, outbreak awareness, decision support, and signal triage.

## Working name
**Epi-Jarvis-Cal**

## Goal
Build an interactive epidemiological monitoring terminal for California that surfaces:
- outbreak and incident signals
- respiratory and enteric disease activity
- wastewater trends
- healthcare pressure
- lab-confirmed surveillance signals
- environmental and vector-borne risk context
- public health alerts and advisories
- county-level situational awareness

## Core design philosophy
This should not be a generic dashboard. It should feel like an **epidemiology operations / intelligence terminal**.

However, unlike conflict/OSINT dashboards, it must be:
- evidence-weighted
- explicit about uncertainty
- careful about lagged data
- resistant to overinterpretation
- useful for real-world epidemiological decision support

## Primary audience
- epidemiologists
- public health analysts
- county/state situational awareness teams
- scientifically literate public-health-adjacent users
- policy and operations users who need fast awareness but not hype

## California-first scope
Prioritize:
- California statewide view
- county-level drill-down
- regional comparison
- cross-county signal detection
- state-specific surveillance programs and reporting cadences

## Product stance
The system should answer:
1. What is happening now?
2. Where is it happening?
3. How unusual is it?
4. How reliable is the signal?
5. What should a public health analyst look at next?

## Non-goals
- not a consumer symptom checker
- not a clinical diagnosis tool
- not an outbreak rumor amplifier
- not a simplistic “top 10 disease threats” widget farm
- not a black-box risk score generator without explainability

## Success criteria
- county/state view is immediately legible
- important epidemiological signals are discoverable within seconds
- lag/coverage/uncertainty are obvious
- users can distinguish alert-worthy events from background variation
- the UI feels operational, not just decorative
